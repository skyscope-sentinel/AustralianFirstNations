### LEGAL ARGUMENTS SUPPORTING THIS PETITION

#### Argument 1: Acknowledgment = Admission of Ownership

**Legal Principle:** *Admission against interest*

When the Commonwealth states "we acknowledge the [Nation] people as Traditional Custodians," it is making a **formal admission** that:
- These people have a pre-existing relationship with the land
- This relationship predates Commonwealth sovereignty claims
- This relationship continues today

**In contract law:** An admission of another's property interest creates an obligation to negotiate fair compensation for use of that property.

**In tort law:** Continued use of another's property without payment constitutes **unjust enrichment**.

#### Argument 2: *Quantum Meruit* - Payment for Value Received

**Common Law Doctrine:**

Where Party A receives a benefit from Party B's property, and Party B has not explicitly waived payment, Party A is **legally obligated** to pay fair value for the benefit received.

**Application:**
- Commonwealth (Party A) benefits from occupation and resource extraction on Traditional Lands (Party B's property)
- Traditional Owners have not waived payment (Native Title claims and land rights activism demonstrate ongoing assertion of economic rights)
- Therefore: Commonwealth **legally owes payment**

#### Argument 3: *Nemo Dat Quod Non Habet* - Cannot Give What You Don't Have

**Legal Maxim:** "One cannot give what one does not have"

**Application:**
- If Commonwealth acknowledges Aboriginal peoples as Traditional Custodians
- Then Commonwealth **did not have absolute ownership to give** when it granted pastoral leases, mining permits, and freehold titles
- Therefore: All such grants are **subject to** the superior Native Title interest
- And: Traditional Owners are entitled to economic benefit from all such grants

#### Argument 4: Constitutional Mandate Under S.51(xxvi)

**Post-1967 Interpretation:**

The 1967 referendum removed discriminatory language and empowered Parliament to make laws **for the benefit of** Aboriginal peoples.

**Application:**
- Economic sovereignty is clearly a "benefit"
- Parliament has **constitutional duty** (not just power) to use s.51(xxvi) for Indigenous benefit
- Failure to legislate economic rights is a **failure to exercise constitutional power** for its intended purpose

#### Argument 5: International Law Obligations

**UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) - Articles 26 & 32**

Australia endorsed UNDRIP in 2009, creating a **legitimate expectation** under administrative law that government will:
- Recognize Indigenous land rights (Article 26)
- Ensure Indigenous control over resources (Article 32)

**In *Minister for Immigration v Teoh* [1995]:** High Court held that international treaty obligations create legitimate expectations that government will comply.

**Application:** Traditional Owners have **legitimate expectation** that Commonwealth will honor UNDRIP through economic recognition.
